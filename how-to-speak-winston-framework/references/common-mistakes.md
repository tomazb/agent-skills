# Common Mistakes Reference

## Related Frameworks
- All frameworks

This reference expands on the Misinterpretation Guards in SKILL.md. Each entry explains the correct interpretation of Winston's advice, why the misinterpretation happens, and how to apply the principle correctly.

## Mistake 1: "Never Use Slides"

**Wrong interpretation:** Winston hates slides. Never use them.

**Correct interpretation:** Winston distinguishes between two media:
- **Boards** (whiteboard, chalkboard) → for *teaching* (building up concepts, step-by-step processes)
- **Slides** → for *exposing* (data, photographs, complex diagrams, evidence)

The mistake is using slides as a script (Crime 4) or as the primary teaching medium when a board would be better. Slides are excellent for showing evidence and data.

**How to apply correctly:** For each section of your talk, ask: "Am I teaching a concept (→ board) or showing evidence (→ slide)?" Most talks need both.

## Mistake 2: "Start With a Joke"

**Wrong interpretation:** Open your talk with humor to warm up the audience.

**Correct interpretation:** Winston explicitly says do NOT open with a joke — the audience isn't warmed up yet. They don't know your style, your rhythm, or whether you're trustworthy. A joke that falls flat in the first 30 seconds is devastating.

However, Winston says DO close with a joke — by the end, the audience IS warmed up. They know you, they like you (if the talk went well), and humor lands naturally.

**The asymmetry is the point:** Jokes require a warmed-up audience. The opening is cold. The closing is warm.

**How to apply correctly:** Open with the Empowerment Promise (Framework 3). Close with a joke or a salute (Framework 10). Never reverse these.

## Mistake 3: "Thank You Is Banned"

**Wrong interpretation:** You must never say "thank you" to an audience.

**Correct interpretation:** "Thank you" is weak as your *final impression* — the literal last words and last slide. It implies the audience endured a burden. It's forgettable. As a final slide, it stays up during all of Q&A, wasting prime visual real estate.

As a casual aside or brief courtesy mid-talk, "thank you" is perfectly fine. The ban is specifically on it being your *closing move*.

**How to apply correctly:**
- Final slide → Contributions (never "thank you")
- Final words → Joke or salute (never "thank you")
- Mid-talk courtesy → Fine ("thank you for that question")

## Mistake 4: "Props Must Be Physical Objects"

**Wrong interpretation:** You need to bring a literal physical object to every talk.

**Correct interpretation:** Winston's props include:
- Physical objects (yes, when available and relevant)
- Board writing (the act of writing IS a prop — it creates movement, engages mirror neurons, and paces delivery)
- Live demonstrations (code execution, physical demos, audience participation)
- Any physical action that makes an abstract concept concrete

The key principle is *physicality* and *mirror neuron activation* — the audience should feel like they're doing the thing, not just watching the thing.

**How to apply correctly:** Ask: "Can I make this concept physical in any way?" Drawing it, building it on a board, demonstrating it live, or using a physical analogy all count.

## Mistake 5: "40pt Font Is Non-Negotiable"

**Wrong interpretation:** Every character on every slide must be exactly 40pt or larger.

**Correct interpretation:** Winston's principle is: make text big enough that it serves the audience, not the speaker's notes. 40pt is a strong heuristic that works in most conference-sized rooms.

Context matters:
- Large venue (100+ people, distant screen) → 40pt minimum makes sense
- Small meeting room (10 people, close screen) → 30pt may be fine
- Code slides → Monospaced at 24-28pt with syntax highlighting may be acceptable if the code is the point

**The underlying principle:** If you need smaller text to fit your content, the content belongs in your verbal delivery, not on the slide. Slides are condiments — support for what you say, not a transcript of what you say.

**How to apply correctly:** Start at 40pt. If content doesn't fit, ask: "Is this essential for the audience to SEE, or can I SAY it?" Move spoken content to speaker notes. Reserve slides for what must be visual.

## Mistake 6: "The Empowerment Promise Is a Table of Contents"

**Wrong interpretation:** Start with "Today I'll cover A, then B, then C."

**Correct interpretation:** The Empowerment Promise is an *outcome commitment*, not an agenda. It tells the audience what they will be *able to do* after the talk, not what topics you'll cover.

**Compare:**
- Agenda (weak): "Today I'll talk about event sourcing, then CQRS, then implementation."
- Empowerment Promise (strong): "By the end of this talk, you'll be able to decide whether event sourcing fits your system and sketch a migration plan."

**How to apply correctly:** Use the format: "By the end of this [talk/session], you will be able to [specific, testable capability]." If the audience can't verify whether the promise was kept, it's too vague.

## Mistake 7: "Contributions Must List Technical Results"

**Wrong interpretation:** The contributions slide is for listing papers, code repos, or deliverables.

**Correct interpretation:** Contributions answer: "What can the AUDIENCE now do?" — not "What did the SPEAKER produce?" Frame every bullet from the audience's perspective.

**Compare:**
- Speaker-centric (weak): "Published 3 papers. Released open-source library. Filed 2 patents."
- Audience-centric (strong): "You can now evaluate event sourcing fit. You have a migration checklist. You've seen a working reference implementation."

**How to apply correctly:** Write each contribution bullet starting with "You" — this forces audience-centric framing.

## Mistake 8: "Questions Mean the Audience Is Confused"

**Wrong interpretation:** If the audience asks questions, the talk failed to explain things clearly.

**Correct interpretation:** Questions are a sign of engagement. Winston's Four Heuristics (Framework 4) explicitly include *asking questions* as a technique to re-engage the audience. Questions during Q&A mean people are thinking about your ideas.

The distinction is between:
- **Clarification questions** ("What did you mean by X?") → your explanation may need work
- **Extension questions** ("What would happen if X?") → your talk sparked thinking — this is success

**How to apply correctly:** Welcome all questions. For clarification questions, note them as signals to improve that section. For extension questions, connect them back to your framework and contributions.

## Quick Reference: Correct vs. Incorrect Application

| Principle | Incorrect | Correct |
|---|---|---|
| Board vs. Slides | Never use slides | Board to teach, slides to expose evidence |
| Opening humor | Start with a joke | Start with Empowerment Promise, close with joke |
| "Thank you" | Never say it | Don't make it your final slide or final words |
| Props | Must be physical objects | Anything that adds physicality (board, demo, object) |
| Font size | 40pt is an absolute rule | 40pt is a strong default; adjust for room size |
| Empowerment Promise | Table of contents | Specific outcome commitment |
| Contributions | Speaker's output list | Audience's new capabilities |
| Audience questions | Sign of failure | Sign of engagement |

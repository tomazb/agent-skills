## The 10 Slide Crimes

### Crime 1: Too Many Slides
**Symptom:** Audience sees you rushing through slides or skipping them entirely.
**Heuristic:** Maximum ~1 slide per 2 minutes of talk time. A 30-minute talk = ~15 slides max.
**Fix:** Merge related slides. Move backup detail to appendix slides (after the contributions slide, available during Q&A).

### Crime 2: Too Many Words Per Slide
**Symptom:** Audience reads the slide instead of listening to you. You become a narrator, not a speaker.
**The Billboard Test:** If the audience can't grasp the slide's message in 3 seconds from the back row, it has too many words.
**Fix:** Extract the key phrase (≤10 words). Move the rest to your speaker notes.

### Crime 3: Font Size Under 40pt
**Symptom:** Squinting from the back rows. Audience disengages because reading requires effort.
**Rule:** 40pt minimum for all text. If it doesn't fit at 40pt, the content belongs in your verbal delivery, not on screen.
**Fix:** Increase font size. If content doesn't fit, split across slides or move to speaker notes. Slides are condiments — they support, not replace.

### Crime 4: Reading Slides Aloud
**Symptom:** Speaker turns to the screen and reads verbatim. Audience thinks "I can read faster than you can talk."
**Fix:** Slides show the visual (diagram, photo, key phrase). You provide the narrative. Never duplicate verbally what's on screen.

### Crime 5: Laser Pointer Dependence
**Symptom:** Red dot bouncing around the screen. Speaker's back to the audience.
**Why it's a crime:** Turning your back breaks eye contact and audience connection. The shaking dot is distracting.
**Fix:** Use built-in slide animations/highlights. Stand next to the screen and point with your hand/arm. Or use a board.

### Crime 6: Standing Far From Slides
**Symptom:** Audience's eyes ping-pong between speaker and screen.
**Fix:** Stand adjacent to the screen. You and the visual should be in the same field of view.

### Crime 7: No White Space
**Symptom:** Every pixel is filled with text, charts, logos, or decoration.
**Why it matters:** White space is cognitive breathing room. Dense slides overwhelm and cause the audience to stop processing.
**Fix:** Remove everything that isn't essential. Default to generous margins. One idea per slide.

### Crime 8: Background Clutter and Logos
**Symptom:** Corporate templates with logos, gradients, footers, page numbers consuming 20-30% of slide real estate.
**Fix:** Plain white or very dark background. No logos (the audience knows who you are). No footers or page numbers.

### Crime 9: Collaborators as Final Slide
**Symptom:** A list of names the audience doesn't know, shown during the most memorable part of the talk.
**Fix:** Move collaborators/acknowledgments to the first or second slide (before the talk begins). The final slide is prime real estate — use it for contributions.

### Crime 10: "Thank You" or "Questions?" as Final Slide
**Symptom:** The slide stays up during all of Q&A. The audience's lasting visual memory is two forgettable words.
**Expanded don'ts:** "Thank you", "Questions?", "The End", "Conclusions", "More details at [URL]", collaborators list.
**Fix:** End on a Contributions slide — a summary of what the talk delivered. This stays up during Q&A and is the last thing anyone photographs.

## The Board Alternative

Not every section needs slides. Consider a board (whiteboard, chalkboard, flip chart) when:
- Building up a concept step by step (the progressive reveal is the teaching)
- Explaining a process or algorithm (hand drawing paces delivery)
- Teaching (mirror neurons activate when the audience sees you write/draw)
- The content benefits from improvisation or audience interaction

**When to keep slides:**
- Showing data, charts, or photographs
- Displaying code that's too long to write live
- Complex diagrams that need precision
- When no board is available

## Final Slide: The Contributions Pattern

The contributions slide answers: "What did this talk give you?"

**Structure:**
- Title: "Contributions" or the talk title restated
- 3-5 bullet points summarizing what the audience gained
- Each bullet mirrors or resolves a point from the empowerment promise
- No names, no URLs, no "thank you"

**Example:**

```text
Understanding Event Sourcing

✓ You can explain event sourcing vs. event-driven architecture
✓ You know when event sourcing helps and when it hurts
✓ You have a checklist for evaluating event sourcing fit
✓ You've seen a working implementation pattern
```

## Audit Walkthrough

To audit an existing deck, go slide by slide and check:

|---|---|---|
| 1 | Total slides ≤ talk minutes ÷ 2 | |
| 2 | Every slide passes the Billboard Test (≤3 seconds) | |
| 3 | All text ≥ 40pt | |
| 4 | No slide is a verbatim script of what you'll say | |
| 5 | No laser pointer needed (highlights built in) | |
| 6 | Speaker position adjacent to screen | |
| 7 | Generous white space on every slide | |
| 8 | No logos, footers, page numbers, or gradients | |
| 9 | Collaborators on slide 1 or 2, not the final slide | |
| 10 | Final slide is Contributions, not "Thank You" | |
